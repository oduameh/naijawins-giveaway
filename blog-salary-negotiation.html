When to Negotiate

New Job Offers

The best time to negotiate is when you have a job offer in hand. The employer has decided they want you‚Äînow it's about terms. Never accept immediately; take time to review and counter if appropriate.

Annual Reviews

Performance review season is the natural time to discuss raises. Come prepared with documented achievements and market data.

After Major Achievements

If you've landed a big client, completed a successful project, or taken on significant new responsibilities, it's a good time to discuss compensation.

When the Market Has Changed

If salaries for your role have increased significantly in the market, or inflation has eroded your purchasing power, you have grounds for renegotiation.

How to Research Market Rates

  • Salary surveys: Jobberman, MyJobMag, and HR consultancies publish salary reports
  • LinkedIn: Connect with peers in similar roles and companies
  • Glassdoor: Check salary reviews from Nigerian companies
  • Recruiters: Speak with industry recruiters about market rates
  • Professional associations: Industry bodies sometimes share compensation data

Negotiation Strategies

1. Know Your Worth

Research market rates for your role, experience level, and industry. Consider your unique skills and achievements that add value.

2. Aim High (But Reasonably)

Start with the top of your range. If you want ‚Ç¶500,000, ask for ‚Ç¶550,000-600,000. There's usually room to meet in the middle.

3. Focus on Value

Frame your request around the value you bring, not your personal needs. "Based on my experience and the market rate..." rather than "I need more because rent increased..."

4. Consider the Full Package

If salary is fixed, negotiate other elements:

  • Signing bonus
  • Performance bonus
  • Housing/transport allowance
  • Health insurance (for family)
  • Pension contributions
  • Leave days
  • Remote work flexibility
  • Professional development budget
  • Car allowance or company car

5. Practice Your Pitch

Rehearse what you'll say. Be confident, professional, and direct. Prepare for counterarguments.

6. Get It in Writing

Once agreed, ensure changes are documented in your offer letter or contract before accepting.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Accepting immediately: Always take time to review offers
  • Revealing your current salary: It anchors negotiations too low
  • Making it personal: Focus on market value, not personal expenses
  • Threatening to leave: Unless you genuinely will, this damages relationships
  • Not having alternatives: Negotiation power comes from options

Frequently Asked Questions

Will negotiating make me look greedy?

No. Professional negotiation is expected and respected. Employers often budget for negotiation and may even question your business acumen if you don't negotiate at all.

Can they withdraw the offer if I negotiate?

This is extremely rare if you negotiate professionally. An employer who withdraws an offer for reasonable negotiation probably wasn't a good employer anyway.

How much should I ask for above the offer?

10-20% is typically reasonable for counter-offers, depending on how far below market the initial offer is. Always be prepared to justify your number.
