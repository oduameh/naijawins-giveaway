Why You Need a Will

Dying without a will (intestate) means Nigerian law decides who gets your assets. This can lead to:

  • Assets going to relatives you didn't intend
  • Surviving spouse not inheriting as expected
  • Lengthy court battles among family members
  • Children from different relationships fighting over assets
  • Customary laws overriding your wishes (especially for women)

How to Write a Valid Will in Nigeria

Requirements

  • Be of sound mind: Understand what you're doing
  • Be at least 21 years old (married women can make wills at 18)
  • Written document: Oral wills are generally not valid
  • Signed by testator: In presence of two witnesses
  • Witnesses: Two adults who are not beneficiaries

What to Include

  1. Your full name, address, and statement of mental capacity
  2. Appointment of executor(s) to administer your estate
  3. List of assets and how they should be distributed
  4. Guardianship provisions for minor children
  5. Residuary clause for any unlisted assets
  6. Signature, date, and witness signatures

Working with an Estate Lawyer

While you can write a basic will yourself, complex estates benefit from professional help:

  • Ensures legal validity

Understanding Trusts

A trust holds assets for the benefit of specified persons. Uses include:

  • Providing for minor children until they're adults
  • Protecting assets from spendthrift heirs
  • Managing assets for disabled beneficiaries
  • Reducing estate disputes
  • Possible tax benefits

Power of Attorney

This document lets someone make decisions on your behalf if you become incapacitated:

  • General POA: Broad financial and legal powers
  • Limited POA: Specific tasks only
  • Healthcare POA: Medical decisions

Frequently Asked Questions

Can my will be challenged?

Yes. Common grounds include lack of mental capacity, undue influence, fraud, or improper execution. A professionally drafted will with medical certification of capacity is harder to challenge.

What if I have children from different relationships?

You can specify exactly how assets should be divided. Without a will, customary laws may favor certain children. Be explicit and consider setting up trusts for minor children.

Should I keep my will secret?

At minimum, your executor should know it exists and where it's kept. Some people share general intentions with family to prevent surprises.
